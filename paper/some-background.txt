Optimal decision trees — including recent advances such as Generalized and Scalable Optimal Sparse Decision Trees (GOSDT) — offer a compelling trade-off: they are fully interpretable, provide globally optimized decision rules, and avoid some of the pathologies of greedy tree induction. High-Stakes Decision Making and the Need for Interpretability

Many consequential policy decisions rely on statistical models whose outputs directly affect financial payments, public reporting, and organizational reputation. Medicare quality measurement programs — including the Hospital Readmissions Reduction Program, Hospital Value-Based Purchasing, and Medicare Advantage Star Ratings — use risk-adjusted outcome models to evaluate provider performance and allocate billions of dollars in incentive payments each year. Because these models determine penalties, bonuses, and public rankings, they operate in a high-stakes regulatory environment where methodological choices must withstand scrutiny from providers, regulators, and the public.

In such settings, interpretability is not merely a desirable feature but often a practical requirement for adoption. Stakeholders frequently demand models whose structure can be clearly explained, audited, and defended, particularly when results may trigger financial penalties or allegations of poor care quality. Historically, this has led agencies such as the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) to rely heavily on relatively transparent approaches — most notably generalized linear models — even when more flexible machine learning methods might offer improved predictive accuracy or better risk adjustment.

The reluctance to deploy complex “black-box” algorithms in regulatory contexts reflects several concerns. First, opaque models can obscure the mechanisms through which risk adjustment occurs, making it difficult for providers to understand how patient characteristics influence performance scores. Second, lack of transparency complicates procedural fairness: organizations may be hesitant to accept payment adjustments generated by models they cannot interpret or replicate. Third, regulatory models must often be documented in technical reports and subjected to public comment, creating institutional pressure toward methods that are explainable and stable.

At the same time, the limitations of traditional parametric models are increasingly apparent. Healthcare data are high-dimensional, nonlinear, and heterogeneous, and misspecified risk adjustment models can distort comparisons across providers — particularly those serving medically complex or socially vulnerable populations. Flexible machine learning approaches offer a pathway toward improved adjustment and more credible causal or performance estimates, but their adoption has been slowed by the interpretability–accuracy trade-off.

Interpretable machine learning methods, and optimal decision trees in particular, provide a promising middle ground. Unlike ensemble methods or deep neural networks, optimal trees produce explicit partitioning rules that can be directly inspected, communicated to stakeholders, and evaluated for face validity. A regulator could, for example, articulate exactly how combinations of clinical characteristics map into predicted risk, allowing providers to understand and potentially contest model behavior. This transparency aligns naturally with the governance requirements of public programs.

However, interpretability alone is insufficient in high-stakes inference problems. Models must also support valid statistical uncertainty quantification, especially when used within causal or semiparametric frameworks such as Double Machine Learning. If interpretable learners fail to achieve the convergence rates required for root-
𝑛
n inference, policymakers may face a trade-off between transparency and statistical validity — an undesirable position when decisions carry large financial and clinical consequences.

This tension motivates the present work. By studying optimal decision trees as nuisance estimators within Double Machine Learning, we aim to develop methods that are simultaneously:

transparent enough for regulatory acceptance,

flexible enough to capture complex risk structure, and

theoretically grounded enough to support valid inference.

Bridging these objectives is particularly important for modern quality measurement, where methodological credibility is inseparable from stakeholder trust. More broadly, establishing interpretable learners with provable statistical guarantees could help expand the toolkit available for high-stakes policy evaluation, enabling agencies to move beyond restrictive parametric models without sacrificing accountability.
